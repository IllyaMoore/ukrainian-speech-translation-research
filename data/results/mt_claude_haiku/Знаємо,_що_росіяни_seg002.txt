On the train was a Ukrainian sergeant, a warrior of the 93rd Separate Mechanized Brigade, Vladyslav Rozhkovskyi, callsign Omar. He did not lose his composure and did everything he could to help others. I want to thank you, Vladyslav. I thank everyone who is doing everything possible and impossible right now to save as many people as possible, as many lives as possible. I spoke today with Tata Kepler, a Ukrainian volunteer who has contributed greatly to the development of our tactical medicine and to the rescue of our warriors. Ukrainian surgeons, Ukrainian doctors are performing miraculous operations, saving lives under the conditions of war. This is experience that needs to be scaled up and that needs to be supported, but not with words, but with decisions and the development of our state institutions. We agreed that Tata, together with active military medics, will provide suggestions on how to provide stronger training for military personnel, specifically from the perspective of medicine. Injuries, combat trauma, the ability to help one's comrades more effectively and faster evacuation. How can we make Ukrainian tactical medicine even stronger? There is much to be done and there is experience that needs to be disseminated. I thank everyone engaged in this work. In particular, the actions of Alpha and what the Security Service is doing quite justly on the territory of the aggressor. In response to all the strikes, in response to all the Russian evil that has come to our land. I am proud of our special forces, and we also discussed control over all processes in the sphere of military production. There must be maximum clarity, and everything Ukraine needs must be produced exactly as Ukraine needs it. Without any chances for Russian sabotage or any internal corruption and the like. This is, in many ways, the responsibility of the Security Service of Ukraine—to ensure real transparency of processes.
